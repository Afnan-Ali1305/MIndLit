Exploring Our Solar System: The Cosmic Neighbourhood

The Solar System is our gravitational home in the vast Milky Way galaxy. It formed roughly 4.6 billion years ago from the collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud. At its absolute heart lies the Sun, a massive yellow dwarf star containing over 99.8% of the entire system's mass.

Closest to the sun lie the rocky terrestrial planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. Mercury is an airless world of extreme temperature shifts, while Venus suffers under a crushing carbon dioxide atmosphere creating a massive runaway greenhouse effect.

Beyond the asteroid belt rest the majestic gas and ice giants: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Jupiter reigns supreme as the largest planet, protecting inner worlds by deflecting dangerous asteroids with its gargantuan size.

Saturn features its iconic ring structure composed of trillions of tiny ice particles and rock chunks orbiting perfectly in line. Uranus and Neptune are cold, deep blue worlds containing strange mixtures of water, ammonia, and methane ice.
